Persia

Persia has been a series of empires that roughly have encompassed the same geographical area since 500 BCE. As different dynasties/ethnic groups took control of the area the empire is classified under different names.

During the time of the Roman Empire, Persia was ruled by the Parthians. The Parthians had restored Persian control after the Greek descendants of Alexander ruled the area, although culturally Parthia was a mix of Greek and Archaic Persian

Parthia was also the only power bordering Rome that proved a substantial threat, but between wars the central location of Parthia between Rome and China as well as India made it an important trade center.

Ardashir I

(224-242)

Revolution

The grandson of a warrior named Sasan (from where the name of the Sassanid dynasty comes), Ardashir was the founder of the new Persian dynasty through means that are contested in various sources. The Parthian Empire was weakend by the Roman invasions of Septimius Severus in 198, and by another war in 216, leaving Parthia weakend militarily. Ardashir from Pars rose in rebellion, although the reasons are unclear; in any case the Battle of Hormozdgan on April 28, 224, Artabanus was killed by Ardashir and the Parthian dynasty was overthrown with his death. Ardashir became the new shah, founding the Sassanid dynasty.

Establishing Control

After taking control Ardashir had to spend several years establishing his control of the old borders of the Parthian Empire. By 230 this brought him to the Roman border with Mesopotamia, which apparently didn't have enough signs posted because he kept invading, eventually fighting Severus Alexander to a stalemate in Armenia. Then Severus Alexander got stabbed by his own soldiers and Ardashir, smelling blood, invaded again in 237, culminating in the taking of the city of Hatra. The city had sort of become independent from the Parthians before Ardashir. It had successfully resisted Roman and Persian attempts to capture it before this attack.

New Order

Ardashir seemed to harshly repudiate the Parthian style of rule, and instead attempted to emulate the older Achaemenids.
