The Provinces of Rome

The Roman Empire was divided into governmental districts called provincia. Seen here at the height of the Empire, the provinces were both added to and lost over time. Public provinces (shown in pink) were, as before under the republic, governed by a proconsul, who was chosen by lot among the ranks of senators who were ex-consuls or ex-praetors, depending on the province assigned. The major imperial provinces (shown in green) were under a legatus Augusti pro praetore, also a senator of consular or praetorian rank. Egypt and some smaller provinces where no legions were based were ruled by a procurator (praefectus in Egypt), whom the emperor selected from non-senators of equestrian rank. The status of a province could change from time to time. In addition, for much of the history of the empire neighboring kingdoms would be forced into treaties that gave Rome effective control, resulting in semi-autonomous client states.

The Republican Provinces

The Homeland

The original homeland of the Roman people, rivalry with Carthage led to expansion in the first Punic War, around 240 BCE.


First united by Rome by around the 260s BCE, The Italian Peninsula was mostly a collection of various subjugated tribes that at first were given legal status inferior to that of Romans. However a particularly bloody civil conflict in 91 BCE erupted over the rights of all Italians to be granted citizenship, which was achieved in 88 BCE. After this point, as the heary of the new empire, Italy became a center for trade and political control in the Mediterranean leading to significant wealth and growth. At it's height, the city of Rome held an estimated 1 million inhabitants, a number that would not be matched by another city until the industrial revolution. As time went on however, the intellectual and agricultural centers in Egypt and the east increasingly resulted in the diversion of resources and people away from the Italian Peninsula gradually dimished the importance of the province.
Settled by Phoenician and later Greek colonists, the island of Sicily was acquired by Rome in 241 BCE except for the independent Kingdom of Syracuse, which was an ally of Rome until its incorporation into the province as the capital in 212 BC at the conclusion of the Second Punic War. For much of the republican period the island would be an important source of grain that fed Italy, until it would be supplanted by the African provinces. Ny the later republic there was pirate activity around the island, which caused a series of expeditions around 70 BCE to be sent to clear them out. In the aftermath of the assassination of Julius Caesar, Sextus Pompey, son of Pompey Magnus, took over the island as well as Corsica and Sardinia and fought in rebellion against Octavian and the Second Triumvirate. He was eventually defeated by 36 BCE. The imperial period has few surviving written sources on Sicily, and this seems to match the archeological evidence that there was a decrease in population of the island. It's fortunes would start to reverse in the 4th century as instability throughout the empire, especially at the frontiers, made such a centrally located island more appealing.
The islands of Corsica and Sardinia were both acquired by Rome at the end of the First Punic War, however the islands' natives woud prove resistant to Roman rule and rebellions would prove a frequent occurence for at least a century. In the aftermath of the assassination of Julius Caesar, Sextus Pompey, son of Pompey Magnus, took over the island as well as Sicily and fought in rebellion against Octavian and the Second Triumvirate. He was eventually defeated by 36 BCE. For much of its incorporation under the Roman Empire the islands would be neglected compared to other areas of the empire, and were frequently used as a place for exile and to send prisoners.

Spain

Originally conquered from Carthage in the Second Punic war in 197 BCE, the Spanish provinces were reorganized by Augustus into 3 parts in 27 BCE


Originally incorporated as Hispania Citerior in the aftermath of the Second Punic War, this region was reorganized by Augustus to include much of the northern and eastern parts of the Iberian peninsula. Originally only the area on the eastern coast was Roman controlled, and it was not until The Cantabrian Wars (29â€“19 BC) brought all of Iberia under Roman domination as the legions moved west. The region was an important mining center for the empire, especially for gold, tin and silver. Its capital was at Tarraco (modern Tarragona, Catalonia).
Originally incorporated as Hispania Ulterior in the aftermath of the Second Punic War, this region was reorganized by Augustus to include the southern region of the Iberian peninsula, more or less around modern day Andalucia. The south of the Iberian peninsula was agriculturally rich, providing for export of wine, olive oil and the fermented fish sauce called garum that were staples of the Mediterranean diet, and its products formed part of the western Mediterranean trade economy even before it submitted to Rome. It remained one of the more stable and Romanized provinces throughout the history of the empire, such that no legion was stationed there.
Lusitania (corresponding loosely with modern Portugal) was not a part of the Iberian peninsula originally taken after the war against Carthage, and would be absorbed through a series of wars in the following century.

North Africa

Much of North Africa was taken by Rome after the Third Punic war in 146 BCE; The western edge in what is today northern Morocco was a longtime client kingdom of Mauretania until about 44 AD when the area was annexed to Rome and divided into two provinces


The western edge of north africa was ruled by the Kingdom of Mauretania until it became a Roman client state in 33 BCE. It was in 40 CE absorbed as a regular province, and although it formed a frontier of the empire in practice the low population on the edge of the Sahara made it a frontier much less turbulent than most of the empire. The principal exports from Mauretania Tingitana were purple dyes and valuable woods. Tingitana also supplied Rome with agricultural goods and animals, such as lions and leopards. The native Mauri were highly regarded and recruited by the Romans as soldiers, especially as light cavalry.
Mauretania Caesariensis was originally part of the same province as Tingitana until being split into a seperate administrative region by emperor Claudius. It shared much of the economic place as its neighbor, however also was a major center of Judaism before 330 CE, as well as one of the centres of the soldier cult of Mithraic mysteries.
Originally the homeland of Carthage until its fall in the Punic wars, Africa became the "granary of the empire", and provided substantial exports of grains, beans, figs, grapes, and other fruits which were central to feeding the city of Rome. As a result Roman Africans enjoyed a high level of prosperity, making it one of the wealthiest provinces in the western part of the Roman empire, second only to Italia. Such growth meant that over time the urban population became increasingly engaged in the craft and service sectors and less in agrarian employment, until a significant portion of the region's vitality came from the sale or trade of products through middlemen to markets in areas both rural and abroad.

Greece

After Alexander the Great died, a power struggle erupted between the many claimants to his empire. Greek connections to Carthage drew Rome's interest, and by 146 BCE Roman forces had occupied pretty much the whole region.


Rome finished the conquest of the ancient kingdom of Macedonia in 148 BC, after a long process of acquiring territory through alliances and wars. Although often overshadowed by other nearby regions like Asia Minor, Macedonia supplied considerablly talented craftspeople as well as tutors, educators and doctors to the Empire.
Roman control of the Achaean peninsula came largely in 146 BC after the defeat of the Achaean League, who sought to resist Roman influence in the region. Educated Greek slaves were much in demand in Rome in the role of doctors and teachers, and educated men were a significant export. Achaea also produced household luxuries, such as furniture, pottery, cosmetics, and linens. Greek olives and olive oil were exported to the rest of the Empire. Copper, lead, and silver mines were exploited in Achaea, though production was not as great as the mines of other Roman-controlled areas. Marble from Greek quarries was a valuable commodity.
The Kingdom of Pergamum had been a client kingdom of Rome until the death of Attalus III without a clear heir in 133 BCE, resulting Rome taking direct control of the province.
