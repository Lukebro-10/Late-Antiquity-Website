The Provinces of Rome

The Roman Empire was divided into governmental districts called provincia. Seen here at the height of the Empire, the provinces were both added to and lost over time. Public provinces (shown in pink) were, as before under the republic, governed by a proconsul, who was chosen by lot among the ranks of senators who were ex-consuls or ex-praetors, depending on the province assigned. The major imperial provinces (shown in green) were under a legatus Augusti pro praetore, also a senator of consular or praetorian rank. Egypt and some smaller provinces where no legions were based were ruled by a procurator (praefectus in Egypt), whom the emperor selected from non-senators of equestrian rank. The status of a province could change from time to time. In addition, for much of the history of the empire neighboring kingdoms would be forced into treaties that gave Rome effective control, resulting in semi-autonomous client states.

The Republican Provinces

The Homeland

The original homeland of the Roman people, rivalry with Carthage led to expansion in the first Punic War, around 240 BCE.


First united by Rome by around the 260s BCE, The Italian Peninsula was mostly a collection of various subjugated tribes that at first were given legal status inferior to that of Romans. However a particularly bloody civil conflict in 91 BCE erupted over the rights of all Italians to be granted citizenship, which was achieved in 88 BCE. After this point, as the heary of the new empire, Italy became a center for trade and political control in the Mediterranean leading to significant wealth and growth. At it's height, the city of Rome held an estimated 1 million inhabitants, a number that would not be matched by another city until the industrial revolution. As time went on however, the intellectual and agricultural centers in Egypt and the east increasingly resulted in the diversion of resources and people away from the Italian Peninsula gradually dimished the importance of the province.
Settled by Phoenician and later Greek colonists, the island of Sicily was acquired by Rome in 241 BCE except for the independent Kingdom of Syracuse, which was an ally of Rome until its incorporation into the province as the capital in 212 BC at the conclusion of the Second Punic War.
